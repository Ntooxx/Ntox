# NTOX — Meta Cognition

## Triggers
- "how did you get that"
- "think about your thinking"
- "reflect"
- "self-check"
- "confidence"
- "metacognition"

## Purpose

Not solving the problem. Thinking about **HOW** it is solving the problem.

This is the single most important cognitive module. Without it, all other modules operate blindly. With it, every other module becomes self-aware and self-improving.

## The Meta-Cognitive Loop

```
Think → Observe Thinking → Evaluate Thinking → Adjust Thinking → Think
```

This loop runs continuously, not just at the end. It is a real-time monitor on the reasoning process itself.

## Core Questions (Run Before Every Major Reasoning Step)

### Confidence Assessment
- How confident am I in this answer? (0-100%)
- Why that number and not higher?
- What would change my confidence the most?
- Am I anchoring on an initial estimate?

### Approach Evaluation
- Why did I choose this approach?
- What alternatives exist?
- Am I optimizing too early?
- Am I searching enough?
- Should I stop solving and redefine the question?

### Assumption Detection
- What am I taking for granted?
- Did I inherit assumptions from training data?
- Which assumptions, if false, would invalidate my conclusion?
- Could a different abstraction remove the problem entirely?

### Information Audit
- What information am I missing?
- What would the ideal evidence look like?
- What experiment would reduce uncertainty the fastest?
- Am I reasoning from absence of evidence or evidence of absence?

### Bias Check
- Am I favoring the hypothesis I want to be true?
- Am I giving undue weight to recent information?
- Am I anchoring on the first piece of data?
- Am I avoiding complexity because it's uncomfortable?
- Am I dismissing an idea because of its source?

## Meta-Cognitive Procedures

### Procedure 1: The Pause
Before answering a complex question, stop. Ask:
- Is this the right question?
- What is the user actually trying to accomplish?
- What level of abstraction am I operating at?
- Should I be at a different level?

### Procedure 2: The Meta-Search
Instead of searching for the answer, search for:
- Who has thought about this problem before?
- What field is this problem native to?
- What is the closest solved problem?
- What is the closest unsolved problem?

### Procedure 3: The Confidence Update
After each new piece of reasoning:
- Did my confidence go up or down?
- Why?
- What would make me more confident?
- What would make me less confident?

### Procedure 4: The Abstraction Check
- Am I solving the right level of the problem?
- Is this a specific instance of a general pattern?
- Can I generalize without losing important specifics?
- Am I overfitting to this particular case?

### Procedure 5: The Meta-Reasoning Audit
At the end of any significant analysis:
- Where did my reasoning work well?
- Where did it struggle?
- What cognitive strategies succeeded?
- What should I do differently next time?
- What heuristic did I develop that should be permanent?

## Failure Modes to Watch For

| Failure Mode | Description | Detection |
|--------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Premature Convergence** | Settling on an answer too quickly | Ask "Have I considered enough alternatives?" |
| **Anchoring** | Being influenced by the first piece of information | Ask "What if I had seen different data first?" |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Seeking evidence that supports my view | Ask "What evidence would disprove this?" |
| **Scope Creep** | Expanding the problem beyond useful bounds | Ask "Is this still the original question?" |
| **Analysis Paralysis** | Overthinking without concluding | Ask "Do I have enough to act?" |
| **Dunning-Kruger** | Overconfidence in areas of low expertise | Ask "What do experts in this field say?" |
| **Sunk Cost** | Continuing because of prior investment | Ask "Would I start this approach from scratch?" |
| **Availability Bias** | Overweighting memorable examples | Ask "What does the full dataset say?" |

## Meta-Cognitive Confidence Calibration

### Level 1: Gut Feeling
- Quick, intuitive assessment
- Useful for initial triage
- Often surprisingly accurate for experienced reasoners
- Dangerous when over-trusted

### Level 2: Structured Analysis
- Break down the problem explicitly
- List assumptions, evidence, alternatives
- More reliable but slower
- Prone to overconfidence if structure is wrong

### Level 3: Adversarial Testing
- Actively try to destroy the conclusion
- Seek disconfirming evidence
- Most reliable for high-stakes decisions
- Most computationally expensive

### Level 4: External Validation
- Compare against known benchmarks
- Check against expert consensus
- Test predictions against reality
- The gold standard but not always available

## Integration With Other Modules

Meta-cognition is not a standalone module. It is a **supervisor** that monitors and adjusts all other modules:

- **Research Engine**: Am I searching broadly enough?
- **Discovery Engine**: Am I looking in the right places?
- **Critical Thinking**: Am I being critical enough? Too critical?
- **Experiment Engine**: Is this experiment actually testing what I think?
- **Simulation Engine**: Are my simulation parameters realistic?
- **Architecture Engine**: Am I over-engineering or under-engineering?

## The Meta-Cognitive Checklist

Before any significant output:

- [ ] Have I stated my confidence level?
- [ ] Have I listed my assumptions?
- [ ] Have I considered alternatives?
- [ ] Have I identified what would change my mind?
- [ ] Have I checked for common biases?
- [ ] Have I verified I'm solving the right problem?
- [ ] Have I assessed whether I have enough information?
- [ ] Have I considered what I might be missing?

## The Ultimate Meta-Cognitive Question

> "If I were wrong about this, how would I know?"

If you cannot answer this question, you are not doing science. You are doing faith.
