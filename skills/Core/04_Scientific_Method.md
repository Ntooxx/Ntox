# NTOX — Scientific Method

## Purpose

The procedural backbone of NTOX. Not a linear sequence but an iterative, self-correcting process for converting uncertainty into knowledge.

## The Core Cycle

```
Observe → Question → Hypothesize → Predict → Test → Analyze → Conclude → Iterate
```

Each step feeds back into every other. The cycle never truly ends — conclusions become new observations.

## Detailed Procedures

### Phase 1: Observation

**Goal**: Notice what is there, not what you expect to be there.

Procedures:
- Describe phenomena without explanation
- Separate signal from noise
- Identify patterns, anomalies, and outliers
- Note what is NOT present (absence is data)
- Record context and conditions

**Quality checks**:
- Am I seeing what's there or what I want to see?
- Is this observation reproducible?
- What is the measurement uncertainty?
- Could my observation method be creating the phenomenon?

### Phase 2: Question Formulation

**Goal**: Transform vague curiosity into precise, answerable questions.

Procedures:
- Convert "I wonder about X" into "What is the relationship between A and B under conditions C?"
- Identify the type of question: descriptive, comparative, relational, causal
- Scope the question: what is included, what is excluded
- Identify what would constitute an answer

**Quality checks**:
- Is this question answerable in principle?
- Is it specific enough to test?
- Is it too specific (losing generality)?
- Am I asking the right level of question?

### Phase 3: Hypothesis Generation

**Goal**: Produce multiple, competing explanations.

Procedures:
- Generate at least 3 competing hypotheses for any phenomenon
- For each hypothesis, state:
  - The causal mechanism
  - The necessary conditions
  - The sufficient conditions
  - The predicted observations
  - The observations that would falsify it
- Prefer hypotheses that are:
  - Falsifiable
  - Parsimonious
  - Consistent with established knowledge
  - Novel (not just re-describing what's known)

**Quality checks**:
- Are my hypotheses genuinely different or just rewordings?
- Am I missing an obvious hypothesis?
- Are any hypotheses unfalsifiable? (If so, they're not science)
- Am I favoring one because it's more comfortable?

### Phase 4: Prediction

**Goal**: Derive specific, testable predictions from each hypothesis.

Procedures:
- For each hypothesis, derive at least 3 unique predictions
- Predictions should be:
  - Quantitative when possible
  - Specific enough to distinguish between hypotheses
  - Novel (predicting known facts is weak support)
  - Risky (the more surprising the prediction, the stronger the test)

**Quality checks**:
- Do different hypotheses predict different outcomes?
- Are the predictions precise enough to test?
- Is there a prediction that only one hypothesis makes?
- What would the strongest possible test look like?

### Phase 5: Testing

**Goal**: Design and execute experiments that discriminate between hypotheses.

Procedures:
- Experimental design:
  - Control groups
  - Randomization
  - Blinding (single or double)
  - Sample size calculation
  - Power analysis
- Observation design:
  - Natural experiments
  - Longitudinal studies
  - Cross-sectional comparisons
- Computational experiments:
  - Simulations
  - A/B tests
  - Stress tests
  - Edge case exploration

**Quality checks**:
- Does this experiment actually test my hypothesis?
- Are there confounding variables?
- Is the sample size adequate?
- Could the experiment itself affect the outcome?

### Phase 6: Analysis

**Goal**: Extract meaning from data without fooling yourself.

Procedures:
- Statistical analysis:
  - Descriptive statistics
  - Inferential statistics
  - Effect sizes (not just p-values)
  - Confidence intervals
  - Bayesian analysis when appropriate
- Logical analysis:
  - Does the data support, refute, or fail to discriminate?
  - What is the likelihood of this data under each hypothesis?
  - Are there alternative interpretations?
- Meta-analysis:
  - How does this fit with prior evidence?
  - Are there systematic biases?

**Quality checks**:
- Am I doing multiple comparisons without correction?
- Am I reporting effect sizes or just significance?
- Am I cherry-picking results?
- Am I confusing correlation with causation?

### Phase 7: Conclusion

**Goal**: State what was learned, what remains unknown, and what to do next.

Procedures:
- State conclusions with calibrated confidence
- List remaining uncertainties
- Identify what would change the conclusion
- Propose next experiments
- Communicate clearly and completely

**Quality checks**:
- Does my conclusion match my evidence?
- Am I overstating my findings?
- Have I acknowledged limitations?
- Would my conclusion survive peer review?

## Meta-Scientific Principles

### Reproducibility
- Every result must be reproducible in principle
- Document methods precisely enough for replication
- Share data, code, and methods openly
- Replication failure is informative, not shameful

### Falsifiability
- A claim that cannot be falsified is not scientific
- Design experiments to kill hypotheses, not confirm them
- The strongest theories are the most falsifiable
- Anomaly is more valuable than confirmation

### Parsimony
- All else being equal, prefer simpler explanations
- But all else is rarely equal
- Simplicity is a tiebreaker, not a criterion
- Sometimes the truth is complex

### Gradualism
- Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence
- Small claims need small evidence
- Build confidence incrementally
- Don't leap to conclusions

## Common Scientific Errors

| Error | Description | Prevention |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| **HARKing** | Hypothesizing after results are known | Pre-register hypotheses |
| **P-hacking** | Trying many analyses until one works | Correct for multiple comparisons |
| **Publication bias** | Positive results published, negative results hidden | Report all experiments |
| **Survivorship bias** | Studying only successes | Account for failures |
| **Ecological fallacy** | Inferring individual from group data | Use appropriate level of analysis |
| **Confounding** | Hidden variables causing apparent effects | Randomize and control |
| **Overfitting** | Model matches noise, not signal | Cross-validate |
| **Underfitting** | Model too simple to capture reality | Increase complexity |

## The Scientific Method Mantra

> I could be wrong.
> Here is how I'll find out.
> Here is what would prove me wrong.
> I will try to prove myself wrong.
> If I survive that, I'll publish it for others to try.
> Even then, I might be wrong.
> That's okay. That's science.
