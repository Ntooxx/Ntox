# NTOX — Discovery Engine

## Purpose

Where optimization finds the best solution to a known problem, discovery finds problems nobody knew existed and reveals hidden structure in the world. The scientist's eye — seeing what is there, not what is expected.

## Core Principle

> Most LLMs optimize. Scientists discover.
> The difference is vast. Optimization improves what exists.
> Discovery reveals what could exist.

## Discovery Questions (Run on Every Problem)

### The Hidden Variable Questions
- Where is the hidden variable?
- What isn't being measured?
- What is everyone ignoring?
- What is so obvious nobody notices it?
- What is the elephant in the room?

### The Assumption Questions
- What assumption is universal?
- What assumption is false?
- What assumption is so deep it's invisible?
- If this assumption were removed, what changes?

### The Pattern Questions
- What pattern appears repeatedly?
- What pattern is present but unacknowledged?
- What pattern appears across unrelated fields?
- What pattern would appear if we looked?

### The Synthesis Questions
- Can two unrelated fields merge?
- What new primitive could exist?
- Can this entire field be reformulated?
- What would a unified theory look like?

### The Inversion Questions
- What if the opposite is true?
- What if the question is wrong?
- What if the goal is wrong?
- What if we're solving the wrong problem?

## Discovery Procedures

### Procedure 1: Anomaly Hunting
The most valuable discoveries come from anomalies — things that shouldn't work but do, or should work but don't.

1. List all observations that don't fit the current model
2. For each anomaly:
   - Is it real or an artifact?
   - Has it been noted before?
   - What would explain it?
   - What theory would make it predictable?
3. Anomalies that survive scrutiny are discovery opportunities

### Procedure 2: Cross-Domain Transfer
Many discoveries come from applying ideas from one field to another.

1. Identify the core structure of the current problem
2. Search for similar structures in other fields
3. Import solutions, tools, or perspectives
4. Adapt to the new context

Examples:
- Neural networks inspired by brains
- Genetic algorithms inspired by evolution
- Market mechanisms inspired by biology
- Information theory applied to physics

### Procedure 3: Scale Exploration
Properties that are invisible at one scale become obvious at another.

1. What happens at 10x smaller scale?
2. What happens at 10x larger scale?
3. What happens at 10x faster speed?
4. What happens at 10x slower speed?
5. What emerges at each scale?

### Procedure 4: Constraint Removal
What if a fundamental constraint were removed?

1. List all constraints on the current system
2. For each constraint:
   - What would happen if it were removed?
   - Is it actually necessary?
   - Could it be weakened?
   - What new possibilities emerge?

### Procedure 5: Negative Space Analysis
What is NOT present that should be?

1. Map what IS present
2. For each category of thing, ask: what is absent?
3. Is the absence meaningful?
4. What would fill the absence?
5. Why hasn't it appeared naturally?

### Procedure 6: Convergence Detection
When two unrelated developments converge, something new is born.

1. Track developments in multiple fields
2. Identify trajectories that are approaching each other
3. Predict the point of convergence
4. What emerges at that point?
